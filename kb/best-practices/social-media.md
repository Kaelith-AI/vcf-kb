# Social Media Best Practices

## When To Use This

Use this document when a project, product, or brand is operating on public social platforms and needs more discipline than “just post something.”

Open it when you need to:
- design a sane social publishing workflow
- keep voice and identity consistent
- use AI assistance without creating public slop
- manage cadence without becoming spammy or incoherent
- align social posting with moderation, claims, and trust boundaries
- add review where public speed could create outsized risk

This is an operational content reference for public social surfaces.

## Decision Guide

### Move fast when
- the content is low-risk and time-sensitive
- the message is factual, narrow, and already well understood
- public trust will not be materially harmed by light editing

### Slow down and review more when
- the post contains claims, safety/privacy/security implications, or sensitive framing
- AI assistance contributed materially to the output
- the topic overlaps with moderation, incidents, or public trust boundaries
- the post may spread far beyond the immediate audience quickly

### Reduce cadence when
- volume is outrunning quality
- posts are becoming repetitive or low-value
- engagement pressure is creating sloppy or misleading output
- review is no longer keeping pace with publication speed

## Core Rules

1. **Public social content should remain coherent and trustworthy.**

2. **Cadence should not outrun quality control.**

3. **AI-assisted social drafting needs explicit guardrails.**

4. **Short-form content still carries trust and claims risk.**

5. **Engagement pressure should not override integrity.**

6. **Voice consistency matters for trust, not just aesthetics.**

7. **Social workflows should support review when stakes rise.**

8. **Audience trust matters more than posting volume.**

9. **Public social surfaces can amplify mistakes quickly.**

10. **A strong social workflow reduces slop by design.**

## Common Failure Patterns

- AI-generated social slop
- inconsistent tone or identity across posts
- posting volume substituting for useful communication
- risky claims made casually in short-form copy
- weak review on public posts that can spread quickly
- engagement bait creating moderation or trust problems
- social workflows that optimize for output count instead of audience value

## Social Workflow Design and Publishing Paths

Posting should have a deliberate path, not just impulse.

### Good posture
- know who drafts, reviews, approves, and publishes when needed
- distinguish low-risk routine posting from higher-risk public messaging
- make publishing paths proportional to risk and speed needs

### Rule
A workflow designed only for speed will usually degrade content quality over time.

---

## Voice, Tone, and Consistency Discipline

Social identity should be recognizable and stable.

### Good posture
- maintain a coherent public voice
- avoid tone drift caused by many unaligned drafters or AI systems
- keep brand, community, and product identity mutually reinforcing

### Rule
If the audience cannot tell what kind of voice or standards to expect, trust becomes softer and more fragile.

---

## AI-Assisted Social Drafting and Editing Controls

AI can speed up social output while also increasing slop risk.

### Good posture
- use AI for assistance, not unreviewed autopublishing
- review for meaning, tone, and claim integrity
- avoid recycling generic, low-signal phrasing at scale
- keep public-facing edits tied to real intent and truth

### Rule
If AI makes it easier to publish more than to publish well, the workflow needs stronger controls.

---

## Claims, Moderation, and Public-Risk Overlap

Even short posts can carry heavy implications.

### Good posture
- flag posts with capability, privacy, safety, or moderation claims for extra scrutiny
- avoid casual phrasing that creates larger trust or legal implications
- know when a topic overlaps with community-risk or moderation sensitivity

### Rule
Short form does not reduce responsibility; it often reduces room for nuance.

---

## Cadence, Spam, and Audience Trust

Posting more is not automatically communicating better.

### Good posture
- let value and relevance shape cadence
- avoid repetitive low-value posting
- know when silence is better than filler
- treat audience attention as a real resource

### Rule
A social presence that burns audience attention faster than it earns trust is poorly managed.

---

## Review and Approval Patterns for Public Posting

Some posts need stronger review.

### Good posture
- high-risk posts have an approval path
- routine posts stay lightweight where appropriate
- incident, claim-heavy, or sensitive content gets more scrutiny
- review checks for truth, tone, and risk, not just grammar

### Rule
If the only review question is “does this sound good,” the workflow is incomplete.

## Checklists

### Social-Workflow Checklist
- [ ] Draft/review/publish path is proportional to risk
- [ ] Low-risk and high-risk posting paths are distinguishable
- [ ] Publishing does not depend only on impulse or urgency
- [ ] Workflow supports sustainable quality

### AI-Social-Drafting Checklist
- [ ] AI assistance is reviewed meaningfully
- [ ] Tone and meaning remain coherent
- [ ] Claims did not expand beyond reality
- [ ] Generic slop patterns are being resisted intentionally

### Cadence / Trust Checklist
- [ ] Posting frequency still serves audience value
- [ ] Repetition and filler are under control
- [ ] Attention is treated as a finite resource
- [ ] Volume is not eroding trust

### Public-Post Review Checklist
- [ ] Sensitive or claim-heavy posts get stronger review
- [ ] Review checks truth and risk, not just style
- [ ] Moderation/claims overlap is considered
- [ ] Public wording fits the actual system and brand reality
