# Legal Claims Messaging Best Practices

## When To Use This

Use this document when public or customer-facing messaging includes capability, privacy, security, compatibility, moderation, or support claims—and especially when stronger phrasing could create trust or legal exposure.

Open it when you need to:
- keep public claims aligned with real system behavior
- avoid overclaiming safety, privacy, or capability
- define support and compatibility language honestly
- distinguish truthful framing from disclaimer theater
- add review triggers for risky public messaging
- keep product copy from outrunning reality

This is a public-claims discipline reference for outward-facing messaging.

## Decision Guide

### Tighten claim review when
- the claim involves safety, privacy, security, AI reliability, or moderation
- the wording could create user reliance or expectation in risky contexts
- support or compatibility language may imply commitments not actually met
- short-form or launch copy is simplifying too aggressively

### Use narrower wording when
- the evidence behind the broader claim is weak
- support depends on conditions that are not universal
- capability varies by context, model, platform, or operator behavior

### Stop and rewrite when
- a disclaimer is being used to rescue misleading framing
- the copy sounds stronger than the system really is
- marketing pressure is overtaking truthfulness
- the team cannot point to concrete evidence behind the promise

---

## Core Rules

1. **Public claims should match actual system behavior.**

2. **Privacy, security, and moderation language must not imply protections that do not exist.**

3. **AI capability language should not outrun reliability reality.**

4. **Compatibility and support claims should be explicit and bounded.**

5. **Disclaimers should not be used to excuse misleading framing.**

6. **Stronger wording is not worth trust or legal exposure.**

7. **Claim review should scale with risk, not only with launch importance.**

8. **Public language should survive contact with the actual product.**

9. **Ambiguity in copy often becomes liability in practice.**

10. **Trustworthy messaging is an operational discipline, not just brand tone.**

---

## Common Failure Patterns

- overclaiming privacy, security, or safety posture
- implying compatibility or support beyond what is really tested
- AI capability language that sounds far more reliable than the system is
- disclaimers added after misleading copy instead of fixing it
- internal hopes or roadmap assumptions leaking into public promises
- short-form copy oversimplifying away critical conditions or limits

---

## Claim Discipline and Overclaim Avoidance

Claims should be proportionate to evidence.

### Good posture
- know what the system actually does
- know what evidence supports each meaningful public promise
- use bounded language when support or reliability varies
- avoid implication creep through tone or omission

### Rule
If the team cannot show why a claim is true, the claim is too strong.

---

## Privacy, Security, and Capability Messaging

These are high-risk claim areas.

### Good posture
- say what protections exist without implying more
- distinguish aspirations from implemented controls
- avoid vague “secure,” “private,” or “safe” language when specifics matter
- make conditions and limits legible where needed

### Rule
High-trust claim categories deserve higher evidence thresholds and narrower phrasing.

---

## AI Limitation and Moderation Messaging

AI systems are easy to overstate.

### Good posture
- describe AI behavior in terms the system can actually sustain
- do not imply universal correctness, safety, or moderation quality
- be honest when humans, review, or escalation remain important
- avoid making provider safety sound like complete system safety

### Rule
If AI messaging makes the human/operator boundary invisible, the claim is likely misleading.

---

## Support and Compatibility Claim Hygiene

Support promises become operational obligations.

### Good posture
- support scope is explicit
- compatibility language matches tested reality
- platform/feature limitations are not hidden inside footnotes only
- “works with” or “supports” language is earned, not hopeful

### Rule
A compatibility claim is a product contract, not a mood.

---

## Disclaimers vs Truthful Framing

Disclaimers are not a cure for misleading copy.

### Good posture
- fix the main framing first
- use disclaimers to clarify real nuance, not to hide contradiction
- keep core messaging truthful even without the footnote

### Rule
If removing the disclaimer makes the claim misleading, the main claim may already be too aggressive.

---

## Legal/Trust Risk Review Triggers

Some messaging should trigger extra review.

### Examples
- health/safety/privacy/security claims
- AI correctness or moderation guarantees
- compatibility or support guarantees
- claims that may materially affect purchase or trust decisions

### Rule
The higher the implied reliance, the stronger the review threshold should be.

---

## Messaging Review Workflows

Good messaging review is a system, not a vibe check.

### Good posture
- claim-heavy copy has explicit review
- reviewers know what to verify against reality
- launch pressure does not bypass risk checks
- revisions preserve truth, not only persuasion

### Rule
If copy review only checks style and not claim truthfulness, the process is incomplete.

## Checklists

### Claim-Evidence Checklist
- [ ] Each strong claim has evidence behind it
- [ ] Wording matches the actual implementation/support boundary
- [ ] Tone is not inflating the promise beyond the facts
- [ ] Claim risk matches review depth

### Privacy/Security Messaging Checklist
- [ ] Protections are described accurately
- [ ] Limits and conditions are not obscured
- [ ] “Secure/private/safe” language is earned and specific enough
- [ ] Messaging does not imply guarantees that do not exist

### AI-Claim Checklist
- [ ] AI capability language is proportionate to reliability
- [ ] Human review/limits are not hidden when they matter
- [ ] Provider safety is not confused with full-system safety
- [ ] The copy would still be fair under edge cases and failure modes

### Support/Compatibility Checklist
- [ ] Compatibility claims are tested and bounded
- [ ] Support scope is explicit
- [ ] Exceptions are not hiding the real story
- [ ] Public wording matches operational reality
